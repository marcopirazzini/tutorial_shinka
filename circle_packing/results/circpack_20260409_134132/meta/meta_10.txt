# GLOBAL INSIGHTS SCRATCHPAD

The following are global insights about optimization approaches and their effectiveness:

## Successful Algorithmic Patterns

• Greedy sequential placement followed by joint nonlinear refinement  
  – Generation 3 (“Greedy+SLSQP Circle Packing”) uses a fast per-circle max-radius greedy pass (minimizing overlap as each circle is placed) and then refines all 26 centres and radii via SLSQP under explicit containment and non-overlap constraints.  
  – Achieved the current best combined score **2.55** (sum of radii = 2.55, radii ∈ [0.05,0.17]).

• Incorporating problem-specific seeds (corners, edges, centre, hex grid)  
  – The best program seeds with 9 strategic positions then a hexagonally-spaced grid, yielding broad initial coverage and avoiding large infeasible gaps.  
  – Similar seeding appears in Generation 1 (jittered hex grid) but without joint SLSQP yields only 2.15.

• Exact constraint enforcement via gradient-based solver  
  – Encoding every wall and circle‐circle constraint as SLSQP “ineq” constraints (distance ≥ sum of radii, centre coordinate ± radius within [0,1]) leads to tightly packed but valid solutions.  
  – This pattern outperforms five-pass shrink heuristics (Gen 0, Gen 2).

• Hybrid two-phase LP + continuous optimization  
  – Generation 4’s inner LP + outer L-BFGS-B multi-start obtains 2.54, very close to the best.  
  – Indicates that alternating exact radius maximization (LP) with centre adjustment (L-BFGS) nearly matches full SLSQP refinement.

## Ineffective Approaches

• Pure simulated annealing with limited restarts or fallback heuristics  
  – Gen 2’s custom annealing + 30-step iterative relaxation stalls at sum = 0.31 and permits zero-radius circles.  
  – Gen 6’s LP+anneal yields 1.68; Gen 8’s LP‐in‐loop annealing 1.98—both suffer from collapsed circles and tight runtime budgets on LP calls.

• Soft-approximation objectives misaligned with hard constraints  
  – Generation 5 (“gradient_driven_packing_refiner”) uses a smooth “soft-min” radius objective then LP.  
  – Resulted in **0.0** (invalid solutions), demonstrating that surrogate objectives must closely match true feasibility criteria.

• Multi-phase refinement without strong initial feasibility  
  – Generation 7’s three seed types + two-phase L-BFGS+B + LP fails all validations.  
  – Overly complex seeding and coarse/fine phases couldn’t guarantee non-overlap, highlighting the need for a reliable first-pass placement.

## Implementation Insights

• Variable flattening and bound setup  
  – Packing variables arranged as [x0,y0,r0,…,xn,yn,rn] with simple bounds (centres ∈ [0,1], radii ∈ [0,0.5]) ease constraint specification and solver performance (as seen in the best program).

• Explicit pairwise constraint loops  
  – Directly iterating over all i<j and adding `dist(ci,cj) – ri – rj ≥ 0` enforces non-overlap precisely, outperforming iterative shrink schemes that only approximate feasibility.

• Greedy max-radius computation  
  – Computing each new circle’s maximal feasible radius in O(idx) by checking wall distances and existing circles guarantees a quick, valid initial layout fed into the global optimizer.

• Tight tolerance and iteration caps  
  – Setting SLSQP’s `ftol=1e-6` and `maxiter=200` balances solution quality vs. runtime, achieving the high 2.55 score without excessive computation.

## Performance Analysis

• Score progression across methods  
  – Ring-based (Gen 0) sum = 1.82 → Heuristic-LP hybrid (Gen 1) 2.15 → Greedy+SLSQP (Gen 3) 2.55, charting clear gains from adding joint nonlinear refinement.

• LP-only vs. mixed solvers  
  – Pure LP center fixes (Gen 4 inner) + L-BFGS gives 2.54; incorporating full SLSQP on all variables edges out to 2.55, underscoring the value of simultaneous variable adjustment.

• Limits of stochastic search  
  – Across Gen 2,6,8,9, pure or LP-coupled simulated annealing plateaus under ~2.0 unless followed by a powerful gradient solver, showing limited utility of random perturbations alone.

• Invalid runs highlight constraint alignment  
  – Gen 5 and Gen 7 (both score 0.0) confirm that heuristic objectives must directly enforce or anticipate final LP constraints to produce valid packings.

# META RECOMMENDATIONS

The following are actionable recommendations for the next program generations:

1. Integrate an inner LP‐radius maximization loop within the SLSQP refinement.  After each SLSQP step (or every N iterations), fix the current centres and solve an LP to maximize all radii (as in Gen 4’s inner loop), then feed the updated radii back into SLSQP.  This alternating LP+nonlinear‐refinement nearly matched the best score and can tighten radii faster while still optimizing centres jointly.  

2. Introduce multi-start SLSQP from jittered strategic seeds.  Create several initial centre/radius arrays by applying small random perturbations (e.g. Gaussian σ=0.02) to the 9 corner/edge/centre seeds plus hexagonal grid.  Run the full SLSQP refinement on each and pick the best sum of radii—this leverages the greedy+SLSQP pattern while escaping local optima via diverse starts.  

3. Supply analytic Jacobians for the containment and non-overlap constraints to SLSQP.  Instead of relying on finite‐difference approximations, implement and pass exact gradients of dist(ci,cj)−ri−rj and ci±ri≤1 constraints.  Empirically, gradient‐aware solvers converge more reliably and can achieve tighter packings under the same iteration/ftol budget.  

4. Add a post-SLSQP reseeding pass that removes the k smallest circles (e.g. k=3–5) and greedily reinserts them using the existing _compute_max_radius_for_circle routine, then reruns SLSQP.  This “partial restart” exploits the greedy placement’s guarantee of feasible first‐pass layouts to fill residual gaps that SLSQP alone may miss.  

5. Enrich the initial seed set with a second, finer hexagonal or low-discrepancy (Sobol) grid layer confined to regions of high slack after the first greedy pass.  Compute wall and neighbour slack per cell of the initial grid, then seed extra centres in the largest-slack cells before greedy radius assignment.  This targeted seeding builds on the success of problem-specific seeds and avoids large infeasible gaps.
