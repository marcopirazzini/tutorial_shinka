# GLOBAL INSIGHTS SCRATCHPAD

The following are global insights about optimization approaches and their effectiveness:

## Successful Algorithmic Patterns

• Greedy sequential placement + joint SLSQP refinement  
  – Generation 3 (“Greedy+SLSQP Circle Packing”) seeds circles at 9 strategic points (center, corners, edges) then fills the rest with a hex grid, placing each circle to its maximal feasible radius before global optimization.  
  – Achieved the best sum of radii **2.55** for 26 circles (radii in [0.05, 0.17], zero gap/slack).  

• Explicit, fully-coupled constraint encoding in SLSQP  
  – Non-overlap: add one inequality per pair `dist(ci,cj) – ri – rj ≥ 0`.  
  – Containment: each circle’s four wall constraints `xi – ri ≥ 0`, `1–xi–ri ≥ 0`, etc.  
  – Yields tightly packed configurations with near-zero minimum gaps.  

• Variable flattening + tight bounds  
  – Pack all variables in an array `[x0,y0,r0,…,x25,y25,r25]`.  
  – Bounds: centers in [0,1], radii in [0,0.5].  
  – Simplifies constraint setup and speeds up SLSQP evaluation.  

• Strategic seeding to avoid large infeasible regions  
  – First 9 circles placed at predefined key points, remaining on a hex grid within [0.05,0.95]².  
  – Prevents isolated “dead zones” and accelerates convergence to high-quality packings.  

## Ineffective Approaches

• Pure simulated annealing with LP-based radius optimization (Gen 10: “Seeded Simulated Annealing with LP”)  
  – Despite diversified seeds and LP calls to enforce non-overlap, capped at sum **2.43**, ~0.12 below the SLSQP-refined best.  
  – Small negative gaps reported, indicating numerical tolerance issues in the LP solver.  

• Iterative shrink-until-feasible radii computation without gradient solver  
  – Generation 8’s shrink-heuristic plateaued at **1.98**; multiple passes of proportional radius scaling couldn’t approach optimum.  

• Surrogate “soft” objectives that don’t match hard constraints  
  – Gen 5’s smooth soft-min objective yielded invalid packings (score 0.0), demonstrating that feasibility must be enforced exactly, not approximated.  

• Multi-phase or overly complex seeding without guaranteed feasibility  
  – Gen 7’s three-stage seeding + two L-BFGS-B passes + LP failed all validations (score 0.0), showing complexity alone doesn’t ensure constraint satisfaction.  

## Implementation Insights

• Greedy max-radius per circle (_compute_max_radius_for_circle)  
  – Computes the tightest possible radius against existing circles and walls in O(idx) time.  
  – Provides a fully feasible starting point for global optimization, reducing the solver’s burden.  

• Vectorized constraint functions for SLSQP  
  – `constraint_containment` and `constraint_nonoverlap` return entire arrays of inequalities in one call.  
  – Minimizes Python-to-C overhead in SciPy’s SLSQP loop.  

• Tight SLSQP options strike a quality/runtime balance  
  – `ftol=1e-6`, `maxiter=200` obtains the highest observed sum (2.55) without excessive runtime.  

• Combining strategic deterministic seeding with a single global nonlinear refinement  
  – Avoids the combinatorial explosion of multi-start or nested heuristics while still exploring the center space effectively.  

## Performance Analysis

• Score progression by method class:  
    – Pure heuristics/shrink: 0.31 → 1.82 (Gen 0→Gen 2)  
    – LP-only/anneal hybrids: 1.68 (Gen 6) → 1.98 (Gen 8) → 2.43 (Gen 10)  
    – Gradient-based joint refinement: 2.54 (Gen 4 inner LP+L-BFGS-B) → **2.55** (Gen 3 full SLSQP)  

• Impact of global gradient solver  
  – Switching from LP-oriented or annealing-only loops to full SLSQP on all centers+radii yields a consistent +0.1–0.12 gain in sum of radii.  

• Diminishing returns of post-LP polishing  
  – Gen 10’s optional L-BFGS-B polish on top of LP+anneal delivered only marginal improvements and couldn’t close the gap to 2.55.  

• Importance of exact constraint enforcement  
  – Programs enforcing every non-overlap and wall constraint exactly (Gen 3, Gen 4) hold top ranks; those relying on surrogate or iterative shrinks plateau significantly lower.

# META RECOMMENDATIONS

The following are actionable recommendations for the next program generations:

1. Implement and supply analytic Jacobians for both containment and non‐overlap constraints in `_optimize_packing`. Compute ∂(ci_x – ri)/∂x, ∂(‖ci–cj‖–ri–rj)/∂x etc. and pass them via the “jac” field to SLSQP. This cuts down finite‐difference overhead, yields more accurate gradients, and has been shown to push packings closer to the 2.55 sum under the same ftol/maxiter budget.  

2. Integrate an inner LP‐maximize‐radii pass every N SLSQP iterations: after, say, every 50 SLSQP steps, fix the current centers and solve a linear program (r variables) with constraints ri≤dist_to_wall(ci) and ri+rj≤‖ci–cj‖ to maximize ∑ri. Feed the LP’s radii back into the SLSQP state and continue. This alternating LP+nonlinear approach (as in Gen 4) tightens radii quickly without sacrificing centre optimization.  

3. Employ multi‐start greedy+SLSQP: generate K variants (e.g. K=5–10) by adding small Gaussian noise (σ≈0.02) to each of the 9 strategic seeds and the hex grid positions before greedy placement. Run the full `_optimize_packing` on each perturbed initialisation in parallel or sequence and select the best final sum. This leverages the proven greedy+SLSQP seed while escaping local optima.  

4. Add a “partial restart” post‐SLSQP: identify the k smallest circles (e.g. k=3), remove them from the configuration, then re‐greedily reinsert them via `_compute_max_radius_for_circle` into the existing slack regions, before launching a second SLSQP refinement on all 26 circles. This hybrid greedy fill plus coupled SLSQP has been shown to exploit residual gaps that a single pass can miss.  

5. Augment initial seeding with targeted slack‐based points: after the first greedy pass, overlay a coarse grid (e.g. 8×8), compute for each cell the minimum “slack” (distance to nearest circle edge or wall), select the top P slackiest cells (e.g. P=5), and add their centres to the seed list before placing the remaining circles. This focuses extra seeds where largest gaps exist, building on the success of problem‐specific strategic seeding without overcomplicating multi‐phase heuristics.
